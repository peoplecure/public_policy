Mapping SNAP (Food Stamps) for Arlington County, Virginia

Introduction: If you have ever visited the Arlington County’s website, you may have noticed that the county’s Economic Independence Division has a winning goal: “Increase SNAP participation rate and amount of SNAP benefits issued to eligible Arlington County residents.” Asides from altruistic reasons, it is economically sensible for local governments to promote SNAP participation for eligible residents.

Before we begin, I want you to know that I am not advocating for a particular public policy. This blog is not about fueling my political ardor. Believe me. I have my opinions about world matters just like you. But, when it comes to public policy, I want to look at things impartially, free from personal feelings and biases as much as possible. My primary goal is to objectively measure government programs and policies for the good of the public. So, if you’re against social policies like food stamps, please hold onto your horses.

That said, why is SNAP economically sensible for local governments? Well, in short-terms, the answer is straightforward. SNAP, formally known as food stamps, is a federal program designed to help low-income earners with groceries. It means that the program is funded by the federal government. When residents spend that federal money in their neighborhood, their regional economic activity increases. And, with the additional economic movement, local governments generate more revenue from taxes.

It isn’t just local governments that can benefit from food stamps. Food stamps are a major source of revenue for many businesses. Take Walmart for example. In 2013, Walmart siphoned nearly 18 percent of all SNAP purchases, which equals to about 14 billion dollars. Even for a company that generates about half a trillion dollars each year, the SNAP program is a lucrative market. So, why would any economist tasked to revitalize their local government not consider promoting SNAP?







Another reason local governments might want to increase SNAP participation rate, albeit a debatable argument, is that of something called marginal propensity to consume (MPC). In the context of our discussion, MPC is simply the proportion of income that people spend on purchasing goods. So, for example, if you spend 75 cents per dollar of your income, then you have a high MPC. On the other hand, if you spend 30 cents per dollar, then you have a low MPC rate.

Since low-income earners have little money, buy inexpensive stuff or live a frugal lifestyle, it may be intuitive to think that low-income earners spend only a small portion of their income on purchasing goods. However, some empirical studies have shown that “households with low cash-on-hand exhibit a much higher MPC than affluent households.” In other words, people who have little money proportionately spend more than the wealthy.

Now, we know that low-income earners proportionately spend most of their income, and SNAP grants cannot be deposited into recipients’ bank accounts as cash. So, theoretically speaking, nearly 100% of the food stamp money returns to the economy when the government injects the food stamp to the poor. This is a reasonable argument. If you don’t believe me, just remember which mega store benefited 14 billion dollars from food stamps in 2013 alone.

As you can see, the public’s sentiment of food stamps is saturated with words like illegal, fraud, fears, criminal, scam, millions, etc. Indeed, SNAP is an expensive program that costs the United States government billions of dollars every year. Just in 2015, we spent $75 billion on SNAP. However, according to the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, an “overwhelming majority of non-disabled, working-age households that start receiving SNAP do not stop working.” In fact, a whopping 96% of households that held jobs before receiving SNAP benefits continued working after receiving SNAP the following year, which is a comparable figure to the national labor turnover rate that spans 1.2% to 7%

Several parts struck out to me in the report. First, the number of applications and beneficiaries have been declining over the past three years. Since a person must submit SNAP application before becoming a recipient, I wanted to see if there was any relationship between the declining number of applications and individuals receiving SNAP benefits. 




cor.test(snap.cs.table.t$'Applications', snap.cs.table.t$'Individual.Recipients')
## 
##  Pearson's product-moment correlation
## 
## data:  snap.cs.table.t$Applications and snap.cs.table.t$Individual.Recipients
## t = 32.592, df = 1, p-value = 0.01953
## alternative hypothesis: true correlation is not equal to 0
## sample estimates:
##       cor 
## 0.9995296
plot(snap.cs.table.t$'Applications', snap.cs.table.t$'Individual.Recipients', xlim=c(2960, 2600), ylim=c(7500, 8400), pch = 20, cex = 1, col = "black", main = "Relationship between Applicants and Individuals", ylab = "Number of Individual Recipients", xlab = "Number of SNAP Applicants",     abline(lm(snap.cs.table.t$'Individual.Recipients' ~ snap.cs.table.t$'Applications'), col="red"))

Data Source: Arlington County, 2017          




This is a perfect correlationship. It is always exciting to see textbook examples happening in the real world. Of course, a correlationship does not imply causation. A drop in the number of applications may not lead to a decrease in the number of SNAP recipients. It is reasonable to estimate an increase in the number of SNAP recipients even when there is a decline in the number of applications. Nonetheless, a perfect correlationship is a fascinating phenomenon. I am going to keep this finding in mind.

Next, I will examine how Arlington County performs compared to the State of Virginia and the nation as a whole. We always have to create a baseline in which a measurement is compared to because, without a baseline, we cannot know whether a measured score is high or low.

Cool, now we know that Arlington County’s SNAP participation rate is significantly lower than the state or national average. We can also see that SNAP rates had increased across the board since the year 2007.

Why is 2007 noteworthy? In December 2007, United States suffered the worst economic downturn since the Great Depression. The financial stress caused by the recession forced millions of people to abandon their homes and businesses, displacing countless families across the nation out into the streets. It was a heartfelt shock that touched many, and some of us still see the world through the broken window that was shattered during the housing market collapse. So yeah, I think the Great Recession had a lot to do with the increase in the food stamp participation.

In addition, we found a discrepancy in the Arlington County’s Performance Plan report and the Census data. Take a look at the Arlington’s data illustrating the SNAP trend of 2014-16 years, then compare that with the trend observed in the above chart. You will notice that according to Arlington County, the number of SNAP recipients have declined over the years. However, in the Census data, the rate of SNAP participation had increased. Unfortunately, 2016 census data is not available at this time (May 2017).

As you can see, when we juxtapose the two reports together in the form of number vs. percent, we learn that the declining trend observed in the number of recipients is not quite right. In actuality, the opposite is correct — the rate of SNAP participation increased.

It may seem like I am nit-picking something trivial for no special reason, but this observation is worth pointing it out. If you take a look at the Arlington County’s SNAP Performance Report, the program purpose says “Increase SNAP participation rate and amount of SNAP benefits issued to eligible Arlington County residents.” Well, I can see how the amount of SNAP benefits have decreased in the form of dollar quantity, but the SNAP benefit rate did not fall

However, it is too early to make any conclusion just yet. SO, enough with my hysteria. I am now interested in knowing why the SNAP participation rate in Arlington County is significantly lower than the rest of the country. Let’s take a look at the economic changes of Arlington County.

The year 2015 was an exception because the Arlington County’s poverty level dramatically declined during that year.

For the administrators at the Economic Independence Division, this is good news. Economically speaking, Arlington County is performing better than the rest of the country. Additionally, after analyzing the poverty and SNAP data using a plot graph, the program purpose found in the SNAP Performance Report coherently and accurately aligns itself to the need of increasing SNAP participation rate.
